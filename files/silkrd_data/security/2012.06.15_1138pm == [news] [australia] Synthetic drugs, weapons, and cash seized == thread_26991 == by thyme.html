Synthetic drugs, weapons and cash seized
CHANELLE SZMOLNIK
15 Jun, 2012 12:00 AM
Quote
MORE than 8.7 kilograms of synthetic cannabis, weapons and cash have been seized by Mount Isa Police and twelve people arrested after a illicit drug crackdown in the city.
The arrests come as part of a joint agency investigation into drug importation in Northern Queensland from Queensland Police, the Australian Federal Police and the Australian Customs and Border Protection Service.
Over the past few days, authorities conducted four search warrants in Mount Isa with 12 people arrested and charged with a range of offence relating to the seizures, according to Mount Isa Police Inspector Paul Biggin.
"We seized more than 8.7kg of synthetic cannabis as well as weapons and cash," Inspector Biggin said.
"They are scheduled to appear before the Mount Isa Magistrates Court on July 2."
He said the arrests were part of a national crackdown on the importation of illicit drugs through the postal system, particularly analogue or synthetic drugs.
"These chemically created substances aim to mimic other illicit substances, giving users the same effect," Inspector Biggin said.
"Since March 2012, 37 people have been arrested (including these latest arrests) in relation to this ongoing operation."
Northern Regional Drug Squad Townsville Acting Detective Senior Sergeant Brad Phelps said the rise in the use and popularity of synthetic drugs in North Queensland is of major concern to Queensland Police.
"This operation has highlighted the prevalence of these drugs in our community," Sen Sgt said.
"The QPS will continue to work in co-operation with our law enforcement partners to target persons involved in the importation and distribution of these drugs in Queensland."
Health authorities say 'designer' drugs pose a serious health risk to the community.
Dr Lucy Burns of the National Drug and Alcohol Research Centre stated that many of these synthetic drugs had chemical profiles were unknown and may have significant adverse health effects.
"Reported harms include heart palpitations, symptoms of anxiety and agitation," Dr Burns said.
"Psychosis and very erratic behaviour have also been reported."
AFP Manager of Crime Operations Peter Sykora stated that the high frequency, low volume nature of these drug imports poses a considerable threat.
"The high income derived from these drugs and the relative low risk to offenders means that this kind of crime has the potential to explode in the community," he said.
According to Police, further arrests have not been ruled out and investigations remain ongoing.
