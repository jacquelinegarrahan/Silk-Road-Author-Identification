Title: Re: I'm new to Silkroad. Any tips to new buyers?
Post by: flaxceed on January 14, 2013, 04:09 am
Generally new buyers have to finalize early [FE].  This means the vendor gets paid before they ship.  I require FE all the time for almost everyone, not just new people.  Look long and hard at a seller's feedback before you buy from them.  When you see a (100) next to a seller's name that is a pretty good indication that they are legit.  Some good ones have (99).  I wouldn't go any lower than that generally or you start to get vendors who are not consistent.  Read over their feedbacks and look for trends like bad products or bad stealth.  Don't just look at the first page of feedbacks!!!!  Go through a bunch of them.

If you need anabolic steroids or diet pills or benzos please look at my profile.  You will see a link in my signature below this post.  Note also that you should read my (or any seller's) entire profile before you order.  There is a damn good reason we go to the effort of writing all of that.  We want to make sure that we are on the same page of music as our buyers.  If there are misunderstandings then our feedback % is in jeopardy, and sellers who value their feedback over and above all else put a lot of time and effort into the profile page and lay down the terms exactly.  We just want to avoid misunderstandings that lead to disgruntled customers.

Most of all have fun!  Silk Road should be a pleasure to use not a scary chore.  Spend time on the forums here and learn all about the logistics of ordering, or just jump right in with a dealer who has a very high % and you will likely have a great experience here on the road.  Best of luck!

@Nightcrawle------Can you  explain why it is so important for a buyer to encrypt their address?

Think of it as an insurance policy of sorts.  The idea behind using PGP/GPG to encrypt one's address is to prevent anyone except the vendor from being able to access the buyer's address information.  This is primarily a precaution against hacking/infiltration of Silk Road. It's a bit like a seatbelt or airbags in a car -- better to have it, and not need it, than need it and not have it.
