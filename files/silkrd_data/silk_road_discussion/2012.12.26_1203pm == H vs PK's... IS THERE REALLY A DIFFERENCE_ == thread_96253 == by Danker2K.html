Post by: eurobilly on December 27, 2012, 02:38 am
Considering this discussion involves correlating the severity of withdrawal illness with the user's perception of the drug's duration/effect on the body, some clarification may be helpful.

* Oxycodone is the generic name for the drug in question.
* Roxicodone is the brand name of the "immediate release or IR" formulation of oxycodone. (IR delivery is generally very crushable, snortable, shootable, abuseable). Since IR oxycodone is now available as a generic, the brand name Roxicodone is rarely dispensed in US pharmacies any longer (insurance companies don't like to pay for expensive brand name drugs after they have come available generically.)
* Oxycontin is the brand name of the "continuous release" formulation of oxycodone. (The "contin" in the name comes from this time-released formulation. You may have seen similar design in other drugs that use the suffix ER/XR-extended release, CR-controlled/continuous release, SR-sustained release, etc. ER delivery is generally more difficult to crush, snort, shoot, abuse.)

There is no drug called "Roxicontin", a common confusion of the drug names mentioned above.

There is a large body of research that exists on the subject of opiate analgesic withdrawal illness. When talking about withdrawal severity, what does "bad" mean to you? Is it the duration of the withdrawal or the force/impact of the symptoms that defines "bad" for you as a user? This is often highly subjective and situational with lots of moving parts; what is bad for one user may be not-so-bad for another. Conversely, what's not-so-bad for one user might mean death to another. Time released products, by design, are meant to give the patient a steady stream of medication all day without having to re-dose. A fentanyl patch (brand name Duragesic) is the ultimate in this approach, giving the patient a full 72 hours of the drug with only a single application. Is fentanyl withdrawal worse than oxycodone IR withdrawal? I will leave that question for the veteran opiate users to answer, knowing full well that there may be some disagreement amongst users in the room. I don't recommend anyone running their own ad-hoc research on themselves just to find out firsthand.

Be careful, friends.
