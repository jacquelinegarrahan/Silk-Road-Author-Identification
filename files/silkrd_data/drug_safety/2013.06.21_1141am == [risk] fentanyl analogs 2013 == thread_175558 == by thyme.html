Recommendations for Laboratory Testing for Acetyl Fentanyl and Patient Evaluation and Treatment for Overdose with Synthetic Opioids Distributed via the CDC Health Alert Network
 June 20, 2013, 15:15 ET 3:15 PM ET CDCHAN-00350
 Summary: Recently, a number of intravenous drug users have overdosed on a new, non-prescription injected synthetic opioid, acetyl fentanyl. Acetyl fentanyl is a fentanyl analog previously undocumented in illicit drug use that is up to five times more potent than heroin. CDC recommends increased vigilance by public health agencies, emergency departments, state laboratories, medical examiners, and coroners for patients with symptoms consistent with opioid overdose and laboratory results showing an enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) positive for fentanyl.
CDC also recommends that public health officials work with laboratories to carry out ELISA screens for fentanyl, and if the results of these screens are positive for fentanyl, conduct gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC/MS) confirmatory testing on specimens to confirm or rule out fentanyl and its analogs, including acetyl fentanyl.
Background  Since March 6, 2013, 14 overdose deaths related to a novel, injected non-prescription synthetic opioid have occurred among intravenous drug users in Rhode Island.  Ten of those deaths occurred in March.  On May 30, 2013, Rhode Island Department of Health confirmed that the implicated synthetic opioid is acetyl fentanyl, a fentanyl analog previously undocumented in illicit drug use.  Acetyl fentanyl is not available as a prescription drug in the U.S.
The age of the persons who died from an acetyl fentanyl overdose ranged from 19 – 57 years, and 10 of the decedents were male.  The toxicology testing results for most of the decedents showed, in addition to acetyl fentanyl, varying mixtures of drugs, including cocaine, heroin (morphine), ethanol, and benzodiazepines.  However, none of these additional substances were present in all decedents and none of these persons tested positive for fentanyl by GC/MS after testing positive for fentanyl by ELISA. Toxicology results for one decedent showed only acetyl fentanyl (by GC/MS) and no other substances.  These deaths represent a significant increase in the number of illicit drug overdose deaths compared with the number of cases typically reported in one month in Rhode Island.
There have been unconfirmed reports from other states of increases in illicit opioid-related overdose events seen in emergency departments.  Media stories have associated these events with “fentanyl-contaminated heroin” or, in some cases, to fentanyl alone.  It is possible that these events are related to acetyl fentanyl, but confirmatory testing is needed.  States other than Rhode Island have not informed CDC that they are testing for acetyl fentanyl.

The Rhode Island Department of Health (HEALTH) has confirmed two additional deaths linked to Acetanilide, n-1-Phenethyl-4-Piperidyl — also known as acetyl fentanyl —an illegal synthetic opiate with properties similar to morphine.
These most recent deaths, which bring the total number of deaths linked to this drug in Rhode Island to 14, occurred on May 26, 2013 in two individuals who were transported from the same residence in southern Rhode Island.
“While final cause of death is still pending further toxicology testing, it is anticipated that acetyl fentanyl will be a significant factor in these deaths,” said Michael Fine, M.D., director of HEALTH.
The Rhode Island State Health Laboratories identified this drug as Acetanilide, n-1-Phenethyl-4-Piperidyl — or, acetyl fentanyl — on May 30, 2013. This drug is not FDA approved, is not commercially available, and is not prescribed by physicians.
The Office of the Medical Examiner initially noted 10 deaths of patients who appear to have died with this substance in their bodies during a time period spanning early March to mid-April, and later confirmed an eleventh death during the same time period, as well as a twelfth related death that occurred on May 16. Overdose cases linked to powerful fentanyl-laced heroin raises alarm bells statewide

A York City Police detective said at this time, there has been no spike in overdose deaths in York County.
By HANNAH SAWYER
Daily Record/Sunday News
Updated:   07/02/2013 12:37:34 AM EDT

York, PA -
Addicts read a cluster of deaths like a calling card, Ken Dickinson said. Word of a dangerous high, like the one produced by the fentanyl-laced heroin now being blamed in six overdose cases in Lebanon County, pulls them into communities. What was meant to be a warning, becomes an invitation.
Dickinson is director of marketing at Gaudenzia, a residential and outpatient drug and alcohol treatment center with 130 programs in Pennsylvania, Maryland and Delaware. He said he first heard of the fentanyl-heroin drug cocktail in 2005, when it appeared on the streets.
Fentanyl is a prescription opiate that is 50 to 100 times more potent than morphine -- the standard by which opiates are measured. It doesn't take much to kill someone, he said.
"There's such a small amount that's needed that there can be mistakes in measuring it out and selling it," he said.
The heroin circulating in Pennsylvania is already some of the "highest potency heroin around," he said, "and now they're adding something to it."
Police monitoring reports
A couple of grains of fentanyl in a bag of heroin -- grains the size of salt -- can cause an overdose, York City Police Detective First Class Andrew Shaffer said.
"With heroin, you have more overdoses than any other illegal narcotic that's out there," Shaffer said. "You just run that risk every time of dying."
Shaffer said his department was aware of the overdoses, including one death, in Lebanon County and an additional two cases in Dauphin County investigators believe are tied to fentanyl use, and they are continuing to monitor reports.
On Thursday, the Pennsylvania Department of Drug and Alcohol Programs issued a statewide warning about fentanyl. According to the news release, the drug has been linked to 50 fatalities in Pennsylvania this year.
The news release called for coroners and medical examiners to screen for the drug in all heroin overdose cases.
At this time, Shaffer said there has been no spike in overdoses in York County. York County Coroner Barry Bloss, who did not have official records available at the time, said he was not aware of any overdose deaths caused by fentanyl.
Memorial Hospital also reported no cases of overdose from fentanyl-laced heroin, and a spokesman for York Hospital said that while there have been recent overdose cases at the hospital, there is no way to determine in the emergency room if the heroin used contained fentanyl.
The Lebanon Daily News reported earlier this month that officials in Lebanon County had taken the unusual step of issuing an official warning to users about the high potency and dangers associated with the drug.
Advising drug users about laced-heroin is part of what is called a "harm reduction program," Dickinson said.
"You pretty much acknowledge the fact that there's going to be people that are going to use these drugs no matter what we tell them. The harm reduction says that I need to educate and inform them," he said.
In some communities, these programs will go so far as to distribute clean needles and syringes to people, he said. Methadone clinics, which keep people from using street drugs, are another example of the harm reduction model.
Shaffer said currently city police have no plans to issue a similar warning about the fentanyl-laced heroin in York.
"It goes without saying: Don't use it," he said. "Our message is and continues to be don't use heroin."
A growing market
Heroin users walk a tightrope between getting the best high possible and dying, Shaffer said.
"They (addicts) push it right up to that limit," he said. Part of fentanyl's appeal is it's ability to help them test those limits, and its potency also contributes to its staying power. Fentanyl appears cyclically, he said. A period of dormancy will be followed by a jump in deaths.
But the addict population is growing, according to Dickinson. It is much larger, and younger, than when the drug first appeared in 2005. The changing demographic could be problematic, increasing the "potential" for more deaths and the introduction of a new generation to fentanyl, he said.
A bloom of young people who were prescribed opiates is to blame, he said. Then regulations changed and doctors became more educated about the drug's effects. Opiates became more expensive, too.
So, they turned to heroin, he said. "The heroin is so plentiful and so cheap that they end up using."
And suppliers seem ready to feed into the burgeoning appetite for strong highs, Shaffer said.
"We've seized more heroin this year ... than I've seen in 18 years," he said. "We're seizing thousands of bags of heroin at a time."
What is fentanyl?
Fentanyl is a prescription narcotic used to relieve severe or chronic pain, commonly used for cancer patients or as a last-resort pain medication. It's available as a skin patch, lozenge, pill, shot, a film that dissolves in the mouth, or intravenously.
As a recreational drug, acetyl fentanyl can often resemble heroin, as it has the same consistency, color and packaging. If a heroin user unknowingly mistakes fentanyl for heroin and takes too much of the drug, the user is at high risk of a fatal overdose.
During the last major fentanyl overdose outbreak in 2006, there were 269 deaths in Philadelphia alone.
Pennsylvania Department of Drug and Alcohol Programs
About Gaudenzia
Gaudenzia is a residential and outpatient drug and alcohol treatment program operating in Pennsylvania, Maryland and Delaware.
Treatment is based on a therapeutic community model, which places emphasis on personal and social responsibility, Ken Dickinson, director of marketing, said. Clients are expected to adhere to certain rules and the "whole milieu of the community becomes the treatment."
Gaudenzia serves more than 17,000 patients annually, including adolescents, mothers and patients with mental illness, according to its website.
