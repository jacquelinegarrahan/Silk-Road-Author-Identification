Title: Re: making edibles out of hash?
Post by: Appa on April 20, 2012, 09:01 am
You can find any recipe that uses cannabis and substitute hash.  Hash is extremely variable as far as potency goes, so I can't advise very well on how much for you to use.  However, it's typically more potent than average weed, and I usually use below-average weed to cook with.  When I do that, I tend to use 0.5-1.5 grams of bud per person.

Crush it up, mix it in with some sort of oil (butter, vegetable oil, olive oil, lard...) and let it stay nice and warm/hot on the stove.  Keep it set LOW, though, you don't want it to be boiling.  Let the cannabinoids extract into the oil for >30 minutes (up to an hour or two).  At this point I would normally strain the weed with a strainer or cheese cloth, but with hash you might just leave it in, I'm not sure.
Anyway, you can now use this oil to cook anything that oil goes into: brownies, cookies, cake, spaghetti, stir fry, spread onto bread...you get the idea.

There's also the option of alcohol extractions/tinctures, but that's a lesson for another day.


Bonus: the best "cannabutter" is made in a slow cooker (Crock Pot).  Just set that thing on "Warm," throw in the butter (actual, unsalted butter, by the stick) and ground up bud.  Stir thoroughly after it melts, then put the lid on.  Leave for 1-3 days to slowly extract at low heat.  You should stir it around every once in a while when you remember it, but each time you open it it's going to reek up your house for hours, so be ready.  After a few days, strain through cheese cloth and use as normal.
The main reason, as far as I understand, that this is better-tasting than a faster method like I described first, has to do mainly with how much chlorophyll/other plant junk gets extracted with the cannabinoids.  At the higher heat of the stove, more chemicals can be extracted, some of which make the butter taste like cannabis or at least "plant-ish" (This is also why I don't recommend the methods that call for adding water to extract out the chlorophyll: some of this chlorophyll-laden water gets trapped in the butter when it separates, and affects the taste.)
Also, high heat allows some stuff (probably mostly oil? not sure) to combust a little, which leaves a sort of burnt taste.  Slow, low heat prevents both of these issues.
