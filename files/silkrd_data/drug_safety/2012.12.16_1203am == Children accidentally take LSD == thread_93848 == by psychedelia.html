Lots of LSD stories in the news lately...


Easton, PA — It apparently turned out to be a very “bad trip” to school. Two brothers, ages 7 and 10, on their way to elementary school reportedly drank liquid LSD from a bottle of breath mint drops that was laying on the ground. By the time they got to the schoolhouse, the two Pennsylvania siblings were “foaming at the mouth and hallucinating” according to AP. They were rushed to the hospital and released the next day, so fortunately it appears they are okay.

The incident occurred on September 24, but yesterday the local prosecutor formally acknowledged that the brothers ingested the hallucinogen (formal name  Lysergic acid diethylamide) after his office conducted an investigation into the horrifying incident involving LSD found on the street.

Northampton County District Attorney John Morganelli also said that police were unable to obtain fingerprints on the bottle to trace it. He added that the boys’ mother and live-in boyfriend tested negative for drugs.

The Foundation for a Drug-Free World explains that dropping acid can cause delusions, hallucinations, panic attacks, impaired time and depth perception, and severe depression among other things. Physical effects can include dilated pupils, high blood pressure, abnormally high or low body temperature, and tremors. Depending upon the dose and other factors, the effects start within  30 minutes could last as long as 12 hours.

According to University of Washington neuroscientist, LSD is still a mystery to some degree even after all these years:

    “LSD has a chemical structure that is very similar to the neurotransmitter called serotonin. It is thought that the effects of LSD are caused by stimulation of serotonin receptors on neurons, perhaps in the brain area called the raphe nuclei. However, it is still not clear what produces all the effects of LSD.”

Foaming =/= drooling excessively.
Foaming is caused by the production of excessive amounts of saliva combined with rapid breathing. When animals get rabies, the virus spreads outwards to the salivary glands, which begin producing large amounts of saliva. The animal's breathing, coupled with excessive saliva production, causes the foam....

November 28, 2012
Two Bangor Area elementary schoolchildren  inadvertently took LSD in September because it was disguised as breath drops they found on their front steps, District Attorney John Morganelli announced today.
Although investigators say they have learned knon drug users lived below the 7-year-old and 10-year-old brothers, Morganelli said police cannot determine how the illegal hallucinogen wound up outside the home in the 500 block of Market Street in Bangor.
No charges will be filed as a result, Morganelli said.
"Although it is quite unfortunate that these young children were exposed to LSD, luckily it appears that there will be no long-term effects," he said. Neither boy has any memory of the incident other than recalling going to the hospital, he said.
The boys' mother, Dawn Connors, told police her sons were late for school Sept. 24 and one had not brushed his teeth. The other boy then pulled out the small breath freshener container and gave him two drops and took one drop himself. The mother then drove the boys to the bus stop, where they boarded their bus without incident, police said.
On the bus, one of the boys became sick and began to vomit. By the time they were taken to the nurse's office, both boys were foaming at the mouth, hallucinating and staring into space, Morganelli said. They were taken to Pocono Medical Center in East Stroudsburg and later transferred to Lehigh Valley Hospital, Salisbury Township, where they were released the next day.
Once recovered, the boys told police they found the bottle on the front steps outside their home and took it inside. Pennsylvania State Police tests determined it contained liquid LSD. The bottle was too contaminated for police to gather any useful investigative information from it, Morganelli said.
Investigators tested Connors and her live-in partner for five weeks, and the Northampton County Division of Children, Youth and Family Services conducted visits to the home. Both adults passed all tests, and the children were never found to be at risk in the home, Morganelli said.
Morganelli said the boys' downstairs neighbors at the time had a history with drugs, but no evidence existed that linked them to the bottle. Both neighbors have since moved out of the house, with one in rehab and the other out of the area. He declined to name the neighbors.
"It's probably not going to be determined at this point. No one is going to volunteer that it was theirs," Morganelli said.
Attempts to contact Connors for comment were not successful.
