That's a *no* on the beta-blockers. Propranolol is a beta-blocker. If the generic name ends in -olol, there's a good chance it's a beta-blocker. 
Not a lot out there on MDPV in particular but, as with other sympathomimetic drugs, the concern is that you can get a potential increase in hypertension due to unopposed alpha-adrenergic effects of the drug, same as cocaine. (This can be a dramatic increase.)
Best option I can think of would be benzodiazepines and a reasonable amount of fluids, option two would be reduced use. 

Hyperthermia / dehydration are common and will further increase heart rate.  (Hyperthermia's something I'd watch, that's the symptom that can escalate into something that gets people actually admitted most often.)

Can you go into cardiac arrest with MDPV alone?  Rapid heart rate, obvious yes; other arrhythmias do indeed show up; fatal arrhythmia, rare, but there is at least one case report published this year with MDPV alone (published in March), and of course the case reports with other drugs.  So, yes, you could. It's very improbable but it is possible.
To be fair, they did resus the guy after cardiac arrest in the March case report and he then died from hyperthermia, rhabdo, coagulopathy, metabolic derangement, and anoxic brain injury, so I guess you could say the arrest itself didn't kill him.

Excited delirium: yes, this is a possibility. Is it an option with small doses? Interesting question, I don't know the answer, my understanding is that it depends on past history / sensitization and maybe some other vulnerabilities.
More than you may want to know on excited delirium: Search for PMC3088378

If you read that, in combination with the brief description of MDPV action below, you can see how concern might arise.

It's like anything else you ingest, it's your choice between the fun quotient and the risk profile.
Overall, from the limited amount out there, there's a dose-response effect and increase in risk.

Apologies if the below abstract is a repost, I'm pretty certain this article's been cited here before and probably discussed to death.
The authors are not being histrionic in their title.
Quote
Neuropsychopharmacology , (17 October 2012) | doi:10.1038/npp.2012.204
Powerful Cocaine-Like Actions of 3,4-Methylenedioxypyrovalerone (MDPV), a Principal Constituent of Psychoactive ‘Bath Salts’ Products
Abstract
The abuse of psychoactive ‘bath salts’ containing cathinones such as 3,4-methylenedioxypyrovalerone (MDPV) is a growing public health concern, yet little is known about their pharmacology. Here, we evaluated the effects of MDPV and related drugs using molecular, cellular, and whole-animal methods. In vitro transporter assays were performed in rat brain synaptosomes and in cells expressing human transporters, while clearance of endogenous dopamine was measured by fast-scan cyclic voltammetry in mouse striatal slices. Assessments of in vivo neurochemistry, locomotor activity, and cardiovascular parameters were carried out in rats. We found that MDPV blocks uptake of [3H]dopamine (IC50=4.1 nm) and [3H]norepinephrine (IC50=26 nm) with high potency but has weak effects on uptake of [3H]serotonin (IC50=3349 nm). In contrast to other psychoactive cathinones (eg, mephedrone), MDPV is not a transporter substrate. The clearance of endogenous dopamine is inhibited by MDPV and cocaine in a similar manner, but MDPV displays greater potency and efficacy. Consistent with in vitro findings, MDPV (0.1–0.3 mg/kg, intravenous) increases extracellular concentrations of dopamine in the nucleus accumbens. Additionally, MDPV (0.1–3.0 mg/kg, subcutaneous) is at least 10 times more potent than cocaine at producing locomotor activation, tachycardia, and hypertension in rats. Our data show that MDPV is a monoamine transporter blocker with increased potency and selectivity for catecholamines when compared with cocaine. The robust stimulation of dopamine transmission by MDPV predicts serious potential for abuse and may provide a mechanism to explain the adverse effects observed in humans taking high doses of ‘bath salts’ preparations.

tl;dr: No, you should not take beta blockers with MDPV to make it more tolerable.
Yes, it's possible to go into an arrhythmia, potentially a fatal one, using MDPV alone.
Yes, excited delirium is a real thing and MDPV has some credible mechanism as a risk for the condition.
Both cardiac arrest and excited delirium are uncommon outcomes of use and appear somewhat (but not entirely) dose-dependent.
