Post by: Ben on June 09, 2013, 01:22 am
The scale mentioned on DX is actually incredibly good value.

Considering how cheap it is, it has amazing resolution, and is also fairly accurate. One thing to keep in mind with cheap scales like these is to verify they are still accurate quite often. You can do so by a reference weight, which is included with the DX scale. The reference is said to be 10 grams, but may read a bit off from that (say, 9.995 or 10.1 grams) which is all fine. Just check that the reference weight keeps giving you the same readout over time.

If you want to compensate for the last percent of error or so, you can weight the reference weight on an analytical balance which will give you its exact mass (this will not be 10.000000 grams). These things are not far off however, i've tried to weigh some of the 10  gram reference weights on proper lab equipment and found them to be very well within 1% of the advertised value.

Bottom line is that these scales are far more accurate compared to, say, the scale you weigh fruits on in a supermarket, and are amazingly cheap. I have no idea on how they manage to make a scale so accurate for 20 bucks, but verification doesn't lie: they just are!
Title: Re: Best Scales
Post by: RobLoblaw on June 09, 2013, 03:39 am
todays market is flooded with digital pocket scales. very cheaply priced which is great but you get a cheap product and inaccuracies in weighing. probably not a huge deal if you plan on weighing ounces cause +/- 100mg isnt a big deal when your buying that much. if your only weighing 100mg, most of the corner store/head shop .01g scales arnt even good enough. not all scales are created equal, not even close when it comes to 3rd world country sweatshops trying to produce the cheapest product possible. i was able to mess around with a high precision scale in a laboratory and we compared 3 scales, all measuring in .01 and they were all inaccurate, obviously your not going to get the same result compared to a top of the line lab scale, but some were better then others. often a scale will be very accurate at a certain point in its range, i guess like a sweet spot and then fluctuate at other points. thats why if you have a .01g-50g scale and you weigh 28g it might be almost dead on but if you weigh 3.5g 8 times and throw it all in the bag you might have 29.4g or something.
if i'm weighing different amounts, i like to have multiple scales for different weights. a set of calibration weights is priceless and you can get nice sets from 10mg-1000g on ebay for under $20. i would get a scale that only weighs to 50g or 100g. the less range, the less inaccuracies. calibrate with a 50g and just use it for 28g weighs. dont use it too weigh 500mg unless you recalibrate with a 500mg weight. never bounce, shake, walk with in pocket or expose to extreme temps. leave in on and always weigh on a flat surface you should have some pretty solid reads. calibrate on a regular basis too.
