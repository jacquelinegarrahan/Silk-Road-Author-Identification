Post by: Notrealperson6708 on May 10, 2013, 05:10 pm
Couple things...

1.)Use your real name.  It will look much more suspicious if you are ordering to your house using a fake name, and it may not even come since the post office has a record of the names that live at certain addresses.
2.)If you don't want to use your home address, use a PO Box.  The only problem with a PO box, is you have to sign up for it, and then go to wherever it is located, and get your package.
3.)Yes, use PGP and stay in escrow ALWAYS.  There is no reason to send your personal information through the web without encryption.
4.)Bitcoins ARE NOT ILLEGAL!!!!  I actually have my personal bank account tied to coinbase, and just send the coins to another wallet before moving them to SR (which in my opinion, seems to be unnecessary....SR tumbles your coins anyway)

I am in no way a pro (only ordered 4 times so far, received 2).  What I've found to be the biggest issue, is finding vendors that aren't going to try and scam you.  The other big issue for me...is the waiting game.  I find myself leaving work every day to go home and check my mail (full of excitement/anxiety), only to find it's not there, then going back to work depressed for the rest of the day.

you don't know that liberte is slightly safer? more anonymous...

I used Liberte all last year. It's a pain to work with, I don't miss it one bit. There's no forums, almost no documentation or support, and you need to be extremely tech savvy to use it properly. Note the keyword, properly. Anyone can boot it, but unless you really know what the fuck you're doing, it's better to find something actively supported IMO.

As far as the more anonymous, the only noticeable difference from what I gathered was the persistent entry guards in Liberte. Some noob with two posts who's asking raw questions isn't exactly Liberte material IMO, he's better off with TAILS, or even just TOR really.

There are hundreds of much-more nuanced discussions in here about the merits of TAILS vs Liberte, and they all come back to persistent entry guards.
