Post by: Shadowdance on June 05, 2013, 04:03 pm
I know a bit about how exotic animals are sold, so here it goes (and the reason it's likely you won't find them on the web).

The big exotics, like lions, tigers, hyenas (which would be AWESOME to have btw) already are being sold by big institutions, they're called zoos.

This system was set up long ago, before the common use of the internet, and the people who play that game are old school, are unlikely to use the web, and can do so safely (legally) at any rate. The laws to discourage exotic animals have only really been passed on the buyers side. Government will not mess with big business.
So this is how it goes. A zoo gets aza certified it has the benefit of appearing like it's helping conservation, and now it can sell it's surplus animals to brokers under the guise of a "breeding program" if the animals are endangered. Brokers, in turn, can sell to anyone they please, if the gov comes around asking why endangered species are being sold, the brokers shrug their shoulders and say, "The animal is on permanent loan". You see in this way, by acting like the animal is part of a breeding program, they become untouchable. Brokers will sell to anyone, they will sell to auctions, to trophy hunters, to pet stores etc.
Also a lot of loopholes, for example, selling tigers across states is illegal. OR IS IT? Hybrid tigers have been deemed legal, so now anyone can cross a bengal and siberian and have generations of cubs to sell, on top of that NO ONE is going to genetically test these animals to make sure they are mixed, so purebred tigers can be sold safely now too.
Small exotics, say like tortoises, are one of the animals completely unprotected by welfare laws. They can be wild caught, rounded up, 50 percent killed in transport, and that's all legal.
The list goes, reptiles, birds, rats, mice, fish and I think guinea pigs, these are not considered animals under the law.
So if one wanted an exotic animal, one should steer clear of the wild caught and try to find auctions, transparent breeders, or better yet a rescue agency, there are tons of tigers, bears, and lions that are "free to good home" now.
Of course, should you get an animal like that you must be able to provide for it properly, and I'm not saying settle for the usda's lowest specifications properly, usda is a joke. You should be able to meet all the psychological and physical needs of the animals, just as you would a dog.

I'll be getting my capybara in a couple of months  8)
ps. If you think want an animal my advice is to accrue some hours working with the type of animal you want, by volunteering somewhere etc. In some states this is a requirement for dangerous animals.
