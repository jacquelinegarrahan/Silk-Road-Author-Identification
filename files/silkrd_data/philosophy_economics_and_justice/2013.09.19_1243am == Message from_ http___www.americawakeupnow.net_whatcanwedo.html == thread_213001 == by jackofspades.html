I think it's kind of naive anytime you start talking about "two kinds of people".  It's 2013, people who don't get at least some information from the internet today are few and far between.  The internet is obviously a better was to learn about the world around you bc access to facts presented by sorces with multiple points of view are so easily accessed.  I'll agree that we need campaign finance reform, and that anyone who'd want the kind of power that we imagine being a president would bring is probably not the most stable kind of person in a lot or respects; however, I believe what is REALLY wrong with American politics (aside from the fact that the institution itself is bloated and views increased productivity at any cost as the only measure of success) is this juvenile idea that there are "two kinds of people" in this world.  Obviously, in American politics that's the two party system.  It's divisive and two sides are polarized on a lot of shit that miss the real issues.

I won't harp too much on capitalism as I believe there's scarcely an alternative, I'm not a Marxist and I think a lot of socialists don't realize that what they are really asking for is to give more power to the people who've already proven their incompetence and inability to govern; though, I do think that holding the value of profit over the value of human lives is also a shitty policy.  I agree with a lot of your sentiments, but I think the only REAL "two kinds of people" are the ones who BELIEVE there are two kinds of people, and those who know better.

As for the OP's question of "what do we do" is absolutely legitimate and it's a question that often keeps me up at night.  It's the reason my wife and I have held of on bringing children into this world: we don't want to be responsible for the suffering of children that the world just plain doesn't have a place for.  I started a thread on "The Wire and Philosophy" on this section of the board and I think part of it applies here: We have a crisis today where institutions are valued above human life.  You've got entire populations who have found themselves without a legitimate place in the economy, in their communities, and in schools.  The people who are supposed to be sounding the alarm: the mainstream media, are all wrapped up in their own little profit games to really do what they are intended to do. 

So, back to "what do we do".  I think efforts to work within any of these established institutions, such as the bullshit, two-party political' behemoth, will easily miss the mark.  These institutions aren't in place to effect any change that is good for the people.  I think our best and brightest hope lies in education.  Plain and simple, the more people who decide that they are a part of this world and take responsibility for educating themselves on how to start behaving in manners that serve not only the individuals interest OR the interest of the group, but both at the same time. 

The "Nash equilibrium" states that the best possible outcome for ALL parties involved in competition is met ONLY when the strategies of all competitors serve the good of BOTH the individual (themselves) AND the group (everyone else):

"Stated simply, Amy and Wili are in Nash equilibrium if Amy is making the best decision she can, taking into account Wili's decision, and Wili is making the best decision he can, taking into account Amy's decision. Likewise, a group of players are in Nash equilibrium if each one is making the best decision that he or she can, taking into account the decisions of the others."

In other words, I believe the heart of the issue at hand is that governing forces in today's society are often established with the intent of either helping the individual or the group as a whole, and often end up helping nobody at all.  Look at a lot of the disillusionment among many politicians, police, teachers, ect who got into their line of work with what probably started out as the best intentions, and now, they realize it's impossible to do any good from within a system that is supposed to serve one and all, and all too often serves only those within-if even then.  I mean, we often look at cops, politicians, "the man" as some "other", but in reality, they're fallible people like anyone else who get wrapped up in these really unnatural social constructs that are flat out worshiped by society.  This starts to get further into economic theory, but I think there are a lot of potential answers in "self-governance" theory.  I think that there are totally other ways we can be living with each other here that aren't popularly considered.
