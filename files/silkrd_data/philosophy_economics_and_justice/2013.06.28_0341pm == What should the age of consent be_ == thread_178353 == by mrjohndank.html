Title: Re: What should the age of consent be?
Post by: thyme on June 29, 2013, 05:17 am
This is like a box of snakes, I know I'm going to regret it... and yet I am drawn in, I have to open the hissing box.

Adolescents can make their other reproductive (and other) health decisions, as a general rule. Adolescents generally are treated as having what's called graduated autonomy for privileges/responsibilities,  but the 'mature minor' concept applies for medical decision-making. The right to decide what actions to take with your body is considered to be essential and fundamental and should be honored as part of treating humans with basic dignity. (Unless it involves ingesting substances, apparently. You can't be trusted with that as an adult.)  Rather than assuming capacity and then examining for diminished capacity when needed, with an adolescent, one assumes 'developing capacity' and then determines the level.

I do not see any compelling reason why sexual autonomy is different from other decisional capacity involving interactions with others and social pressure.
If someone can make the treatment decisions for sequelae of sex, it is bizarre to say they do not have the ability to consent to sex in the first place. Restricting the sexual behavior of 'mature minors' based on the predatory behavior of some (immature) adults makes as much sense to me as restricting the drug-taking behavior of adults based on the predatory behavior of some people in the drug-taking/selling world. ["Some people have been robbed or killed in transactions involving drugs, therefore all drug-use must be bad and illegal."]


We use arbitrary ages for certain things such as 'age of consent' because the majority of people hit these developmental milestones on a predictable timeline.  The 'rule of sevens' is a classic arbitrary rule. A child under 7 can't provide informed consent, can't formulate criminal plans/thought, can't be negligent. From 7 to 14, a minor is considered to lack capacity, but we start to ask them for consent; it's a question of 'developing' capacity, not diminished capacity. From 14 to 18/21, they are considered capable of consent, and often are considered responsible for criminal or negligent conduct. Depending on the crime, from 14-17, they may be considered adult, depending on evaluation and jurisdiction.

Very few 12 year olds will truly meet the standards of decisional capacity, which I am, with effort, refraining from outlining. They also lack autonomy in making decisions and will defer to the adult (they will deny this tooth-and-nail; objectively,  when you look at behavior, they defer to the adult/authority).  Sweeping generalizations follow: Humans start differentiating and making their 'own' decisions independent of adult influence around 13, most are differentiated by 15 (although peer influence becomes much more potent). By 16 they (fairly) reliably display autonomy. 
Reacting with the 'reflexive no' is not really an autonomous response - 14 year olds tend to start here and move into more actual autonomy by 15. Most 15 year olds can meet the cognitive standards for consent, and 16+ year olds should, otherwise they go into the narrow end of the bell curve.
Where it gets weird is peer influence, gist reasoning, blah blah blah. [But you know what? I don't hold an adult to those standards. "Is that person unduly influenced by their peers to marry?" I look at the elements of capacity, and executive autonomy, and see if they're met, and I move the hell on.]

So...

TL;DR, since there's no poll:
16 -  sure.
15 - could live with it. It's not ideal but I'd rather in this case default on the side of independence and the fact that normal developmental process is, uh, normal.
14 - I just... I've interviewed too many 14 year olds lately. There are exceptions but they're rare. Can't live with 14.
13 - Nope. They're not there yet.
